Death Valley National Park

Death Valley National Park is an American national park that straddles the California – Nevada border, east of the Sierra Nevada[^1^][13]. The park boundaries include Death Valley, the northern section of Panamint Valley, the southern section of Eureka Valley, and most of Saline Valley[^1^][13]. The park occupies an interface zone between the arid Great Basin and Mojave deserts, protecting the northwest corner of the Mojave Desert and its diverse environment of salt-flats, sand dunes, badlands, valleys, canyons, and mountains[^1^][13].

Location and History

Death Valley is a desert valley in Eastern California, in the northern Mojave Desert, bordering the Great Basin Desert[^2^][8]. It is the largest national park in the contiguous United States, as well as the hottest, driest and lowest of all the national parks in the United States[^1^][13]. It contains Badwater Basin, the second-lowest point in the Western Hemisphere and lowest in North America at 282 feet (86 m) below sea level[^1^][13]. A series of Native American groups inhabited the area from as early as 7000 BC, most recently the Timbisha around 1000 AD who migrated between winter camps in the valleys and summer grounds in the mountains[^1^][13]. A group of European Americans, trapped in the valley in 1849 while looking for a shortcut to the gold fields of California, gave the valley its name, even though only one of their group died there[^1^][13].

Major Attractions

Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes

Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes are a must-see attraction in Death Valley National Park. Named after the mesquite tree, the area’s dunes are created by the wind which steadily moves the sand and the mountains, which keep the sand from being blown too far[^3^][18].

![Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes](https://bing.com/th?id=OIP.eHBWChQIksnuTPMm-IDnWQHaFu)

Zabriskie Point

Zabriskie Point is a part of Amargosa Range located in east of Death Valley noted for its erosional landscape. It is composed of sediments from Furnace Creek Lake, which dried up 5 million years ago—long before Death Valley came into existence[^4^][9].

![Zabriskie Point](https://bing.com/th?id=OIP.PKdQ2xaaccNF5_OsuDBx3AHaFA)

Badwater Basin

Badwater Basin is an endorheic basin in Death Valley National Park, noted as the lowest point in North America, with a depth of 282 ft (86 m) below sea level[^1^][13].

![Badwater Basin](https://bing.com/th?id=OIP.8xRLAayeDlBMbz3hheKohAAAAA)

Devil's Golf Course

Devil's Golf Course is a large salt pan on the floor of Death Valley, located in the Mojave Desert within Death Valley National Park. The park is noted for its towering peaks frosted with winter snow, rare rainstorms that bring vast fields of wildflowers[^4^][9].

![Devil's Golf Course](https://bing.com/th?id=OIP.psn9mBT69iRG2l1aRjKXjgHaEQ)

Racetrack Playa

Racetrack Playa is a scenic dry lake feature with "sailing stones" that inscribe linear "racetrack" imprints[^4^][9].

![Racetrack Playa](https://bing.com/th?id=OIP.zWI2gE_8qjG7mprf0FONugHaE8)

Dante's View

Dante's View is a viewpoint terrace at 1,669 m (5,476 ft) height, on the north side of Coffin Peak, along the crest of the Black Mountains, overlooking Death Valley[^4^][9].
