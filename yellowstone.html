Introduction

Yellowstone National Park, established in 1872, is located primarily in the U.S. state of Wyoming, although it also extends into Montana and Idaho. Covering nearly 3,500 square miles, Yellowstone is renowned for its geothermal wonders, wildlife diversity, and breathtaking landscapes.

The park sits atop one of the largest active volcanic systems in the world, resulting in a unique landscape dotted with geysers, hot springs, mud pots, and fumaroles. It is also home to diverse ecosystems, including subalpine forests, grasslands, and alpine meadows.

Visitors come from all over the globe to experience Yellowstone's natural wonders, from the iconic Old Faithful Geyser to the majestic wildlife that roams its vast wilderness.

Major Attractions

Old Faithful Geyser

Old Faithful is one of the most famous geysers in the world, known for its highly predictable eruptions. Located in the Upper Geyser Basin, it shoots water up to 185 feet into the air, drawing crowds of visitors who gather to witness its spectacular displays. Eruptions typically occur every 60 to 110 minutes, making it an iconic symbol of Yellowstone's geothermal wonders.

Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone

The Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone is a breathtaking canyon carved by the Yellowstone River. Spanning approximately 20 miles long, the canyon showcases vibrant hues of yellow, orange, and pink, created by the presence of hydrothermal activity and iron-rich rocks. Visitors can enjoy stunning viewpoints such as Artist Point and Inspiration Point, offering panoramic vistas of the canyon and the powerful Lower Falls plunging 308 feet into the gorge.

Mammoth Hot Springs

Mammoth Hot Springs features unique terraces of travertine formed by the hot springs' mineral deposits. Located in the northern part of the park, these terraces resemble frozen waterfalls, with intricate patterns and vibrant colors created by the interaction of water, heat, and underground minerals. Visitors can explore boardwalks winding through this otherworldly landscape, marveling at the constantly changing thermal features and the diverse microbial life thriving in the extreme conditions.

Norris Geyser Basin

Norris Geyser Basin is the hottest and most dynamic thermal area in Yellowstone. It features a variety of geothermal features, including geysers, hot springs, fumaroles, and mud pots. Steamboat Geyser, the world's tallest active geyser, is located here and can erupt to heights of over 300 feet. Visitors can explore the basin via boardwalks, experiencing the ever-changing landscape shaped by the park's volcanic activity.

Yellowstone Lake

Yellowstone Lake is the largest high-elevation lake in North America and one of the most iconic features of the park. Covering over 130 square miles, the lake offers opportunities for boating, fishing, and wildlife viewing. Its pristine waters are surrounded by rugged mountains and provide habitat for a variety of species, including cutthroat trout, bald eagles, and grizzly bears.

Wildlife

Yellowstone is home to an incredible array of wildlife, including grizzly bears, black bears, wolves, bison, elk, moose, and bighorn sheep. The park's diverse habitats support these animals, offering ample opportunities for wildlife viewing and photography. Lamar Valley, often referred to as the "Serengeti of North America," is one of the best places in the park to observe wildlife, particularly wolves and bison.

Hayden Valley

Hayden Valley is another prime wildlife viewing area in Yellowstone, known for its wide-open spaces and abundant wildlife. Bison herds are a common sight here, along with elk, grizzly bears, and various bird species. The valley is bisected by the Yellowstone River, providing a picturesque backdrop for observing wildlife in their natural habitat.

Tower Fall

Tower Fall is a stunning waterfall located in the northeastern part of the park. Dropping approximately 132 feet over volcanic cliffs, the waterfall is framed by towering rock formations, creating a dramatic and picturesque scene. Visitors can view the waterfall from a viewpoint near the parking area or hike down to the base for a closer look.

Mount Washburn

Mount Washburn is one of the most popular hiking destinations in Yellowstone, offering panoramic views of the surrounding landscape. The summit sits at an elevation of 10,243 feet and can be reached via a moderately challenging trail. Hikers are rewarded with sweeping vistas of Yellowstone Lake, the Absaroka Range, and the Hayden Valley.
